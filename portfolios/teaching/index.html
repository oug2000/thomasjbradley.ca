Teaching Portfolio

I want to inspire the passion for learning and creating in others.

Philosophy

I believe that everybody should perpetually learn, try, build, break, and fix things. In the ever-changing interactive field, learning is an important skill; I want to inspire the passion for learning and creating in others. As a twenty-first and a half century professor, I have two major roles: curator and mentor.

Read my whole teaching philosophy…

Curator

We now carry the sum of human knowledge in our pockets. And as amazing as that is, there is still a major problem: we carry the sum of human knowledge in our pockets—that’s a lot of stuff.

With that much stuff it’s daunting to determine which information is relevant, up-to-date, and correct. With fast-paced interactive practices, the problems are compounded. Information gets out-dated really quickly, best practices change rapidly, there’s incorrect details, and huge amounts of knowledge.

When tackling a new topic I browse, read, and try many different resources. Books, tutorials, articles, magazines, code; anything to help me understand. My learning process is funnel-like. I gather countless pieces of information: wrong pieces, right pieces, old pieces, new pieces, contradictory pieces, best practices, out-dated practices—anything that seems relevant—and I funnel it into a cohesive understanding.

As an educator, I believe it’s my job to help the students by funnelling the sum of human knowledge and simplifying their information gathering. I point my students to the most appropriate resources for a specific topic. For the interactive field, generally those resources are online because books become old quickly—though I recognize books are helpful for many learners.

In the interactive field there is so much to know, even after funnelling the information, it can still be overwhelming. We all need reminders of the material and I aim to have the material available to the students online, so they can refer to it inside, and outside, the classroom as needed.

Mentor

Humans learn at different speeds and in different ways and I strive to help each learner the way that is best for them. As a mentor I can understand each individual’s world view and uniquely tailor the learning experience.

As a teacher in the interactive field, it’s important to follow and understand all the best practices and know when and how to use them. Having a T-shaped knowledge of the interactive field allows me to guide students down the path that is uniquely appropriate and helpful.

Knowledge experiences don’t always happen in the classroom and I encourage students to learn through the exploration of practical examples that portray a specific skill or piece of information critical to a thorough understanding. I want everyone to get as much knowledge—and experience—as they want, and I do so by providing supplemental resources and direction for students as a group and individually.

It’s important to guide learners to the answer of their problems not give them the answer; breaking things and fixing things is an important learning process. I want to give students the tools they need and point them in the right direction.

Goals

I believe that I should give students knowledge blocks that they can rearrange and piece together into a cohesive whole. Like Lego, I give the students small connecting pieces and they must use their knowledge and imagination to connect the pieces together and build something amazing.

This strong foundation of blocks, coupled with the students’ imagination, encourages them to continue their learning outside the classroom. It promotes learners to explore and research the connections and expand their understanding using the learning style that is most effective for them.

I strive to inspire the love for learning in everybody. Perpetual learning is important for the constant evolving interactive world.

Online Tests

I built a couple fun, online games for my students to test their understanding of a few important topics. The materials were presented online through reading articles, videos, and experience using the tools. The tests were then administered in class.

From the start, I wanted to make a fun learning experience and it was important to me to contribute back to the web education community. I decided to make the tests open source so they could be used by other instructors.

Keyboard Commander

Keyboard Commander tests understanding of keyboard use. There are two layers to the test: recalling keyboard shortcuts, and navigating the browser by only using the keyboard. The game can be a little tricky because any mouse movement will trigger game over and force the user to start again.

The test was light and fun and students really enjoyed it—even though it was difficult. One surprising experience was between students who were seated beside each other: one accidentally hit the other’s mouse, causing the game to restart. There was some light-hearted yelling and the class had a good laugh.

Play Keyboard Commander ⇢
Dev Tool Dynamo

Dev Tool Dynamo tests understanding of browser tools that help developers debug their code. The game gets users to complete a series of challenges using the browser developer tools.

I had originally thought the gamified test wasn’t as fun or exciting as Keyboard Commander but many of the students enjoyed it more. The primary feedback was to hold the test earlier in the term because many students learned new functions that would have helped them with their projects.

Evolving Schedules

Teaching web design and development presents an interesting challenge: the material is a moving target. Not only does the material need to be updated per term based on student feedback, but it also must be updated to reflect current industry trends and practices. I actively follow and participate in the web community to make sure my materials are always relevant to the industry.

Read more about my evolving schedules…

MTM 1519 Web Development, part of the Interactive Multimedia Program, is meant to introduce new learners to the World Wide Web and how to create interactive, usable, and accessible websites. It is primarily scoped as a code-based class, where students write markup and code to create their web projects.

First Time Teaching MTM 1519

In my first time teaching the course, Fall, 2010, I split the material into two main segments: HTML and CSS, & Javascript. The introduction of Javascript in the final third of the term was too drastic of a change making the material very difficult to grasp. My next iteration, Winter, 2011, dropped Javascript from the curriculum to concentrate on HTML and CSS. This ended up being more effective for the students by decreasing the amount of material for the term while allowing them to have more practice with HTML and CSS.

During my first two runs of the course, HTML was segregated into two versions: XHTML and HTML5. At the time HTML5 wasn’t supported in enough browsers to be teaching as the sole version of HTML. Beginning in Fall, 2011, HTML5 is now the only version I teach.

More Low Level Material

From watching the students and understanding what aspects of programming were difficult, the assignments changed and adapted to their needs. I introduced a few more low-level–type assignments on understanding file paths and code organization. I originally had hoped the students would pick the material up from watching me use the techniques, but came to the realization that explanation and reinforcement was needed. The introduction of more low-level material was helpful to students at the beginning, but practice—and reminder—is still required.

More WordPress

Starting in Fall, 2011, the course made another large change: the introduction of WordPress into the curriculum. In the previous years the client projects became more geared towards providing content management systems and WordPress was the appropriate choice. Students were having to learn and implement WordPress themselves, so it was decided to include it in the course material.

The introduction of WordPress presented a problem very similar to Javascript in the first go: there are almost two courses happening in the same term. I decided to introduce WordPress immediately after mid-term to provide enough time for a thorough understanding. WordPress is a complex and time-consuming product to implement and in attempting to prepare the students for their client projects, I introduced too many concepts the first time teaching it, confusing and frustrating the students. In the second time teaching it, Winter, 2012, I reduced the material and the students now seem more engaged and even willing to do the extra material as bonus marks since it is now a choice instead of a requirement.

Sample Assignments

The major goal for all the homework I assign is to have the students create something practical. I want learners to take the information they’ve gained in class, and independently, and apply it in different ways to get a thorough understanding. The best way to learn code is to try and fail; the debugging process is invaluable.

Portfolio, Graphic Design

The portfolio project has one specific goal: for the Graphic Design students to create an effective online portfolio to help them get an internship. It was a cross-course project. In DSN 1598 Usability, the students planned and designed their portfolios. In my course, DSN 1668 Design for Interactive IV, the students code and implemented the portfolio.

The requirements for the portfolio were technical, helping enforce the best practices learned throughout the term and allowing the students to explore content and design that is appropriate to them. The project was extremely successful and every student produced exactly what they needed.

Open Data App, Interactive Multimedia

The Open Data App is an online application with a few learning goals:

  1. Apply knowledge of database and server-side development
  2. Regularly use a version control system
  3. Participate in a long-term project with many deliverables and specific requirements
  4. Break, debug, and fix the application in unexpected ways

The Open Data application was successful though very difficult. I originally had much higher expectations for what could be accomplished, but from student feedback, I reduced the requirements and turned many of them into bonus challenges. Interestingly, many learners opted to complete a few of the bonus challenges.

I think the portfolio site was one of the most important projects in the entire program because it's really important for designers to have websites. It made getting a field placement much easier and it continues to act as self promotion post-graduation. The process of coding a site from start to finish improved my skills to the point where I can now offer this service to clients.

Open Data App, by Trish Dupelle, Interactive Multimedia

The open data application project was delivered by Professor Thomas Bradley in my second semester in the Interactive Multimedia Program at Algonquin College. In general, the project was a long but worthy process that was facilitated with Thomas’ extensive and patient help along the way.

Feedback from Learners

Feedback from learners is extremely important to improve and adapt the course for everybody. I encourage students to discuss any problems they are having with the materials and assignments so we can come up with something more appropriate and compatible with their learning styles.

Feedback reflection…

There is one piece of feedback that consistently shows up and I struggle to fix it: I teach too quickly. Many of my lessons are centred around code tutorials, where students follow along and type the code samples on their own computers. The major challenge is that it’s difficult to keep pace with my typing while watching and listening.

I’m actively trying many different approaches to accommodate everyone.

  1. Take plenty of breaks between coding batches to allow people to catch-up.
  2. I try to provide the complete materials before and afterwards.
  3. I allow students to just listen and watch during class and type the code later.

The problem with some of these approaches is that it doesn’t encourage learners to retype the code, but re-typing code is extremely helpful in learning.
